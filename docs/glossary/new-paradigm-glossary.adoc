== Purpose

This glossary defines working terms used across the New Paradigm cycle and the public canon documents.

The definitions are not final academic definitions. They are working language for a developing documentation system.

== Survival mode

A state or environment in which threat, scarcity, status defense, urgent resource protection, or group loyalty becomes the main editor of perception and action.

Survival mode is not bad in itself. It is necessary for real danger. The problem begins when it becomes the default architecture of culture, economy, media, or technology.

== Truth mode

A way of organizing decisions in which verification matters more than reaction, procedure matters more than shouting, evidence matters more than belonging, and long-term sustainability matters more than immediate victory.

Truth mode does not require perfect certainty. It requires a usable path toward correction.

== Threat interface

A behavioral interface in which reality is primarily experienced through danger, urgency, escape, defense, accusation, or control.

In threat interface, the mind often asks: how do I reduce danger now?

== Decision interface

A structured environment that helps a person or institution move from uncertainty to action through fact, risk, option, and action.

A good decision interface reduces panic without hiding reality.

== Fact, Risk, Option, Action

A minimum decision pattern:

* Fact: what is known?
* Risk: what may happen?
* Option: what paths are available?
* Action: what next step reduces uncertainty?

== Belonging filter

The social filter through which a person evaluates whether a source is safe, familiar, loyal, hostile, foreign, authoritative, or threatening before fully evaluating the claim itself.

== Truth-oriented belonging

A form of belonging in which identity does not require the rejection of verification.

It allows a person or group to say: we remain connected, and we can still correct ourselves.

== Inner narrator

The part of personal or collective cognition that creates a coherent explanation after a decision, reaction, or position has already been shaped by hidden motives, pressure, fear, reward, status, or belonging.

The inner narrator is not useless. It creates continuity. But without verification, it can protect error.

== Decision trace

An inspectable record of how a decision was formed.

A minimum decision trace preserves fact, reason, alternative, doubt, and verification.

== Punishment signal

A signal that mainly records failure, delay, default, noncompliance, risk, penalty, or exclusion.

Punishment signals may be necessary for accountability, but they cannot by themselves build a mature economy of trust.

== Contribution signal

A positive, contextual, verifiable signal that records participation, support, fulfillment, repair, coordination, verification, or shared value creation.

A contribution signal should open upside. It must not become a hidden punishment mechanism.

== Contribution ledger

A contextual record of contribution-related events: support, promise, fulfillment, dispute, repair, receipt, verification, and impact.

A contribution ledger should not be treated as a universal moral ranking of people.

== Contribution score

A possible computed signal derived from contribution records inside a defined context.

It must not be used as a credit score, social credit score, hidden blacklist, or standalone basis for high-impact decisions.

== Impact receipt

A record showing that a person, group, or organization participated in an event that created, supported, repaired, verified, or delivered value.

== Community dividend

A long-horizon concept in which a portion of value created by a community, platform, treasury, or contribution network may be distributed back to participants.

This is a conceptual term, not a promise of income, entitlement, yield, or investment return.

== Public canon

The public documentation layer that records principles, boundaries, vocabulary, architectural intent, and responsible interpretation of the project direction.
