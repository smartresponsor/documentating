== Abstract

The Contribution Ledger is a proposed economic memory layer for recording verifiable participation, support, fulfillment, repair, dispute resolution, and impact.

The central idea is that modern systems remember failure more easily than contribution. They remember default, delay, noncompliance, risk, and penalty. They often forget what a person helped create, support, deliver, verify, coordinate, or repair.

A contribution ledger does not replace accountability. It complements accountability with a positive, contextual, inspectable memory of useful participation.

== Problem

Many existing economic and institutional systems are punishment-heavy.

They are strong at recording risk and weak at preserving positive contribution. This creates a behavioral environment where people learn to avoid loss, hide weakness, fear correction, and treat institutions as threat.

The missing layer is not a universal moral score. The missing layer is a contextual record of contribution that can be inspected, corrected, and connected to future opportunities.

== Core concept

The Contribution Ledger records events such as:

* support;
* pledge;
* contribution;
* promise;
* fulfillment;
* delivery;
* dispute;
* repair;
* verification;
* impact receipt.

Each event should preserve enough context to remain explainable:

* who participated;
* what was promised;
* what was delivered;
* what evidence exists;
* what uncertainty remains;
* what dispute or correction occurred;
* what changed because of the contribution.

== Relationship to credit scoring

Credit scoring is a useful historical analogy because it shows how a decision market can form around standardized trust signals.

But the Contribution Ledger is not a credit score.

A credit score generally measures repayment risk in a lending context. A contribution ledger records positive participation and fulfillment in defined contexts.

The goal is not to decide whether a person deserves credit, housing, employment, insurance, healthcare, or basic rights. The goal is to make useful contribution visible without turning human value into a number.

== Relationship to crowdfunding

Crowdfunding is a natural entry point because it begins with trust before full proof.

A backer supports something that is not fully delivered yet. A creator makes a promise. A reward may be offered. A community forms around an uncertain future outcome.

That structure creates a strong need for contribution memory:

* who supported early;
* what reward was promised;
* what was fulfilled;
* what was delayed;
* what was disputed;
* what was repaired;
* what impact resulted.

== Relationship to reward-commerce

Reward-commerce treats a reward or souvenir not only as a product, but as a participation receipt.

A physical or digital reward can say: this person participated in making something possible.

The item is not only a transaction artifact. It is a record of belonging to a project, campaign, community, or shared outcome.

== Relationship to community dividend

A long-horizon contribution system may support community dividend experiments.

A community dividend means that part of the value generated by a community, platform, treasury, sponsor pool, or contribution network may be returned to participants.

This document does not define an income promise, yield product, token right, or investment return.

The safer first goal is to record contribution and simulate possible distributions before any real financial mechanism is proposed.

== AI role

AI can help structure contribution records.

It can assist with:

* summarizing campaign promises;
* detecting missing evidence;
* classifying fulfillment status;
* identifying dispute patterns;
* explaining why a signal changed;
* preserving decision trace;
* comparing stated promise and delivered outcome.

AI must not become a final authority over human dignity. It should support trace, not replace accountability.

== Ledger role

A ledger can preserve integrity and auditability.

At the early stage, this does not require a public blockchain. The first practical ledger may be a conventional append-only audit trail with verifiable timestamps, event types, source references, and correction records.

Blockchain or cryptographic receipts may become relevant later where public verification, portability, or tamper resistance becomes necessary.

The system must not become:

* a second credit score;
* a denial mechanism for basic access;
* an AI judge;
* a speculative income promise.

== First practical wedge

The first practical wedge should be narrow:

* crowdfunding campaign;
* creator promise;
* support event;
* reward promise;
* fulfillment event;
* dispute event;
* repair event;
* impact receipt;
* contextual trust passport.

This allows the system to test contribution memory without claiming to solve the entire economy.

== Open questions

The model still requires research on:

* privacy boundaries;
* dispute procedure;
* explainability;
* score misuse prevention;
* data portability;
* governance rights;
* regulatory classification;
* anti-fraud controls;
* community dividend feasibility;
* AI verification limits.

== Current conclusion

The Contribution Ledger should be understood as a memory layer for proven participation, not as a universal score of human value.

Its first responsibility is to help systems remember contribution with trace, context, correction, and dignity.
