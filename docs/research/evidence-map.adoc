== Draft status

This is an unpublished research source draft.

It is stored to preserve the research structure behind the public article cycle and future white paper. It is not yet part of the public Antora navigation.

This document is a research map, not a final literature review.

== Purpose

The Evidence Map collects the major research and source categories needed to support the contribution-oriented economic narrative.

The goal is to prevent the project from becoming only a philosophical statement. The public narrative should be supported by history, regulation, behavioral science, technical architecture, and governance constraints.

== Research areas

The current evidence map is organized into these areas:

* human perception and action;
* reward systems and motivation;
* threat mode and uncertainty;
* belonging and social cognition;
* post-factum explanation and decision trace;
* credit bureaus and credit scoring history;
* crowdfunding and reward obligations;
* basic income and community dividend models;
* AI decision support and explainability;
* ledger integrity and auditability;
* governance, privacy, and fairness;
* anti-social-credit boundaries.

== Historical analogy: credit bureaus

Research questions:

* How did credit reporting agencies become trusted decision infrastructure?
* What was the original small responsibility they solved?
* How did local reporting become standardized national infrastructure?
* Which parts of this history are useful as analogy?
* Which parts must not be copied because they created exclusion, opacity, or harm?

Working conclusion:

Credit bureaus show how a decision market can form around standardized trust signals. But contribution-oriented reputation must not become a second credit score.

== Crowdfunding

Research questions:

* What trust problems appear in reward-based crowdfunding?
* What happens when rewards are delayed, changed, or never fulfilled?
* What evidence do supporters need before and after contributing?
* What do creators need to demonstrate good faith and fulfillment?
* How can disputes and repairs be recorded without creating permanent punishment?

Working conclusion:

Crowdfunding is a strong first wedge because it begins with trust before completion and naturally creates promises, rewards, receipts, fulfillment, disputes, and impact records.

== Reward-commerce

Research questions:

* When is a reward just a product?
* When does a reward become a participation receipt?
* How can a physical or digital souvenir preserve a record of contribution?
* How can reward-commerce avoid becoming merely another store?

Working conclusion:

Reward-commerce can connect emotional participation, economic transaction, and contribution memory.

== Basic income and community dividend

Research questions:

* What is the difference between universal basic income, community dividend, and platform-based redistribution?
* What models have been proposed or tested?
* Which claims are too risky for early publication?
* How can a community dividend be discussed without promising income, yield, or entitlement?

Working conclusion:

Community dividend should remain a long-horizon research concept until there is a clear legal, economic, and governance structure.

== AI and explanation

Research questions:

* How can AI help structure facts, risks, options, and actions?
* How can AI preserve decision trace instead of producing fluent justification?
* How can AI explanations avoid becoming external inner narrators for institutions?
* What human review boundaries are necessary?

Working conclusion:

AI should belong to the architecture of trace, not only the architecture of answer.

== Ledger and auditability

Research questions:

* What records require append-only audit trail?
* Which records can remain private, contextual, or temporary?
* When is conventional audit logging enough?
* When might cryptographic receipts or blockchain become useful?
* How can corrections and disputes be preserved without erasing history or creating permanent stigma?

Working conclusion:

The first version should not depend on blockchain. It should begin with a pragmatic, inspectable audit trail.

== Governance and safety

Research questions:

* How can the system avoid becoming social credit?
* What high-impact decisions must be excluded?
* What rights to explanation, dispute, and correction are required?
* How can negative records be bounded and proportional?
* How can privacy be protected while preserving verification?

Working conclusion:

Governance is not an appendix. It is part of the core architecture.

== Evidence needed before public white paper maturity

Before the full white paper becomes public-facing, the project should collect stronger sources for:

* history of credit reporting;
* credit score regulation and consumer reporting boundaries;
* crowdfunding risk and reward fulfillment;
* behavioral science of threat, belonging, and post-factum explanation;
* AI explainability and decision support;
* privacy and data minimization;
* fairness and high-impact automated decision risks;
* UBI and community dividend experiments;
* ledger/audit trail patterns.

== Open questions

Open questions include:

* Should the first public product object be called Trust Passport, Contribution Passport, or Campaign Trust Record?
* Should Contribution Score be avoided as a public term until governance is mature?
* How much of the system should be visible to users versus auditors?
* What correction and dispute process is proportionate for early pilots?
* What event types are safe to record?
* Which event types should never be part of a score?
* What is the smallest pilot that tests trust without creating regulated risk?

== Current conclusion

The Evidence Map is the parking place for research discipline.

It should grow behind the article cycle and support later publication of the white paper, governance charter, and product wedge.
