== Purpose

Crowdfunding Trust Passport is the first practical wedge for testing contribution-based reputation without attempting to build a universal economic system.

The goal is narrow: create an inspectable trust and fulfillment record around crowdfunding-like campaigns and reward-commerce flows.

== Why crowdfunding

Crowdfunding is a trust event before completion.

A supporter gives value before the final result exists. A creator makes a promise. A community forms around uncertainty. A reward may later become both a product and a participation receipt.

That makes crowdfunding a strong starting point for a contribution ledger.

== Core problem

Crowdfunding environments often lack a strong memory of:

* what was promised;
* what was funded;
* what was delivered;
* what was delayed;
* what was disputed;
* what was repaired;
* what contribution helped make possible.

Supporters need trust. Creators need a way to demonstrate fulfillment. Sponsors need a way to inspect impact. Communities need a way to remember participation.

== Product object

A Trust Passport is not a universal score of a person.

It is a contextual record around a campaign, creator, supporter, reward, and fulfillment history.

It should answer:

* What campaign was supported?
* What was promised?
* What was the support event?
* What reward or receipt was attached?
* What was fulfilled?
* What was disputed?
* What was repaired?
* What evidence supports the status?

== Core flow

The first flow is:

* campaign created;
* promise declared;
* supporter participates;
* reward promise is recorded;
* contribution receipt is issued;
* fulfillment is tracked;
* dispute or delay is recorded if needed;
* repair or resolution is recorded;
* trust passport is updated.

== Main entities

The first version may use these entities:

* Campaign;
* Creator;
* Supporter;
* Contribution;
* Reward;
* Promise;
* Fulfillment;
* Dispute;
* Repair;
* Impact Receipt;
* Trust Passport;
* Decision Trace.

== What the passport can show

The passport can show contextual indicators such as:

* campaign status;
* promise clarity;
* support volume;
* fulfillment status;
* unresolved disputes;
* repair history;
* evidence completeness;
* participation receipts;
* creator fulfillment pattern;
* supporter participation history inside the same context.

These indicators should remain explainable and contextual.

== What it must not become

The Trust Passport must not become:

* a hidden blacklist;
* a universal person score;
* a credit score;
* a social credit system;
* a tool for denying basic rights;
* a permanent stain for failed projects;
* an AI-only judgment system.

Failure, delay, and dispute must be contextual and correctable.

== MVP boundary

The MVP should avoid:

* tokens;
* dividends;
* securities-like language;
* automated universal scoring;
* permanent reputation labels;
* high-impact eligibility decisions.

The MVP should focus on records, receipts, status, evidence, and explanation.

== Initial screens or outputs

A minimal product may expose:

* campaign trust summary;
* promise and reward table;
* fulfillment timeline;
* dispute and repair log;
* supporter participation receipt;
* creator fulfillment record;
* decision trace panel;
* exportable campaign evidence pack.

== AI assistance

AI can help summarize and structure:

* campaign promises;
* reward obligations;
* fulfillment updates;
* dispute messages;
* missing evidence;
* inconsistency between promise and delivery;
* decision trace explanations.

AI should not decide final trust status without human review in disputed cases.

== First success criteria

A first pilot should test whether the passport improves:

* supporter understanding;
* creator accountability;
* sponsor confidence;
* dispute clarity;
* evidence completeness;
* fulfillment transparency;
* trust without overclaiming.

== Current conclusion

Crowdfunding Trust Passport is the safest first wedge because it tests contribution memory in a bounded context.

It connects crowdfunding, reward-commerce, decision trace, and contribution ledger without immediately claiming to build a universal score or income system.
