Toward a Formal Statement of Dynamic Non-Identity

Source asciidoc: `docs/article/toward-a-formal-statement-of-dynamic-non-identity.adoc` == Purpose

This note translates the hypothesis of dynamic non-identity into a preliminary formal vocabulary.

It is not a proof. It is a bridge between manifesto language and mathematical language.

The goal is to identify what would need to be specified before the hypothesis could become more than a philosophical statement.

Informal Hypothesis

The informal hypothesis is:

Two initially equal driving factors do not preserve absolute dynamic identity over time in an open system unless the conditions preserving that identity remain symmetrical.

This can be developed through the concepts of state, equivalence, time evolution, perturbation, and preservation.

Objects of Discussion

Let S be a system.

Let A and B be two distinguishable dynamic factors within or related to S.

A and B may represent forces, influences, interactions, constraints, gradients, instabilities, or other driving factors, depending on the chosen model.

Let t denote time, or a parameter playing the role of temporal ordering.

Let x_A(t) and x_B(t) denote the relevant states or parameter-vectors of A and B at time t.

Equality at a Moment

A and B may be equal at time t under a selected equivalence relation E.

This may be written informally as:

\$E(x_A(t), x_B(t)) = true\$

This does not mean A and B are absolutely identical.

It only means that they are equivalent under the selected relation E.

Examples of E may include:

  • equality of magnitude;

  • equality of direction;

  • equality of energy contribution;

  • equality of a selected vector component;

  • equality of a modeled role;

  • equality of a defined state class.

Dynamic Identity Over an Interval

Dynamic identity over time is stronger.

For an interval I = [t0, t1], strong dynamic identity would require:

\$for all t in I: E_strong(x_A(t), x_B(t)) = true\$

Here E_strong is not merely equality of one measurement. It may include relations, context, exposure, constraints, histories, and transition behavior.

The stronger E becomes, the more demanding its preservation becomes.

Preservation Condition

The key formal insight is:

Equivalence over time requires preservation of the conditions that make the equivalence valid.

Let C_A(t) and C_B(t) represent the relevant conditions around A and B.

These conditions may include:

  • boundary conditions;

  • relations to other factors;

  • perturbation exposure;

  • constraints;

  • environmental inputs;

  • histories of interaction;

  • rules of state transition.

If C_A and C_B remain symmetrical with respect to E, then E may be preserved.

If C_A and C_B diverge in a way relevant to E, then E may fail.

Open-System Disturbance

Let P_A(t) and P_B(t) denote perturbations or external influences affecting A and B.

In a perfectly symmetrical case:

\$P_A(t) = P_B(t)\$

with respect to the relevant model.

In an open system, however, there is no automatic guarantee that:

\$P_A(t) == P_B(t) for all t\$

A minimal disturbance condition may be stated as:

If there exists a time t in I such that the relevant perturbation, relation, or boundary condition affecting A differs from that affecting B, then strong dynamic identity requires an additional preservation mechanism.

Without such a mechanism, identity becomes contingent rather than absolute.

Weak Claim

The weak version of the hypothesis is:

In an open system, equality at a moment does not imply dynamic identity over an interval.

This is safe and modest.

It is almost definitional once equality and dynamic identity are distinguished.

Medium Claim

The medium version is:

In an open system, preservation of dynamic identity over time requires preservation of the relevant symmetry conditions.

This claim is stronger but still disciplined.

It says that identity is conditional, not automatic.

Strong Claim

The strong version is:

In real open systems, absolute dynamic identity between two distinct driving factors over long intervals is not an ordinary stable reality, but a limiting abstraction.

This is the philosophical-scientific heart of the hypothesis.

It is not yet a theorem. To become formal, it would need a specified class of systems, perturbations, equivalence relations, and preservation conditions.

Possible Formal Statement

A first formal statement may be written in prose:

For two distinguishable dynamic factors A and B in an open system S, equality under a selected equivalence relation E at time t0 does not entail equality under a stronger dynamic equivalence relation E_strong over interval I. Preservation of E_strong requires preservation of the relevant symmetry conditions C that define the equivalence. In the absence of such preserved conditions, strong dynamic identity is not guaranteed and should be treated as a limiting idealization.

Conditional Version

A conditional version is safer:

If two dynamic factors are exposed to non-identical relevant perturbations or non-identical relevant relations over time, and if the system lacks a mechanism that cancels or absorbs those differences, then strong dynamic identity between those factors will not be preserved.

This version avoids overclaiming.

It allows stable systems, attractors, feedback, and conservation structures to exist.

Statistical Version

A statistical version may be possible:

For many classes of open systems, the probability that two distinguishable factors preserve total dynamic identity over long intervals decreases as the number of relevant uncontrolled perturbation channels increases.

This statement would require real mathematical development.

It may connect the hypothesis to probability, statistical mechanics, stochastic processes, and information theory.

Relation to Stability

The hypothesis must distinguish identity from stability.

A system may be stable without preserving absolute identity between two factors.

A perturbation may be absorbed. A trajectory may return to an attractor. A structure may remain recognizable. But this does not necessarily mean that two dynamic factors remained absolutely identical in all relevant properties.

Therefore:

Stability is not the same as absolute identity.

This distinction is essential.

Relation to Invariants

The hypothesis must also respect invariants.

Some quantities can remain invariant under transformations. This is not a problem for the hypothesis.

The hypothesis does not say invariants are impossible. It says that total dynamic identity of distinguishable factors is stronger than preservation of a selected invariant.

Therefore:

Invariant preservation is compatible with dynamic non-identity.

Two factors may share an invariant while differing in context, trajectory, exposure, or relation.

Relation to Equivalence Classes

A useful mathematical framing is equivalence class membership.

A and B may belong to the same equivalence class under E at time t.

But under a stronger relation E_strong, or at a later time, they may not.

This suggests a hierarchy:

  • equality of one value;

  • equivalence under a relation;

  • equivalence under a class of transformations;

  • preservation of equivalence under time evolution;

  • total dynamic identity.

The hypothesis mainly concerns the final levels of this hierarchy.

What Must Be Developed Next

A complete formalization would require:

  1. a defined class of systems;

  2. a definition of driving factor;

  3. a state representation;

  4. a distinction between weak and strong equivalence;

  5. a definition of openness;

  6. a definition of relevant perturbation;

  7. a preservation condition;

  8. a theorem, proposition, or conditional argument;

  9. examples and counterexamples.

Candidate Proposition

A candidate proposition for future work:

Given two distinguishable dynamic factors A and B in an open system S, if A and B are equal under E at t0 but are subject to non-equivalent relevant conditions C_A and C_B over interval I, then E_strong(A, B) over I cannot be assumed without an explicit preservation mechanism.

This is intentionally modest.

It is a bridge toward rigor, not a final law.

Candidate Research Question

The main research question becomes:

Under what conditions can initially equal dynamic factors preserve strong identity over time, and when does openness make that preservation impossible, unstable, or only idealized?

This question can be investigated philosophically, mathematically, and through examples from dynamic systems.

Closing Statement

The formal heart of dynamic non-identity is not the denial of equality.

It is the distinction between equality at a moment and identity over time.

Equality is parameter-bound. Identity over time is condition-bound. In open systems, conditions are exposed to difference.

Therefore, the burden of proof belongs to any claim of absolute dynamic identity, not to the ordinary expectation of divergence, asymmetry, or contextual difference.
