Glossary of Dynamic Non-Absoluteness

Source asciidoc: `docs/article/glossary-of-dynamic-non-absoluteness.adoc` == Purpose

This glossary defines the controlled vocabulary of the Dynamic Non-Absoluteness research program.

Its purpose is not to freeze the hypothesis permanently. Its purpose is to prevent future documents from using the same words in incompatible ways.

The hypothesis depends on terms that can easily become ambiguous: force, equality, identity, absolute, open system, perturbation, symmetry, and preservation. This glossary gives each term a working meaning.

Absolute

In this program, absolute means more than strong, stable, large, long-lasting, or fundamental.

A factor is treated as absolute only if it is assumed to be:

  • fully isolated from other influences;

  • constant across the relevant interval;

  • fully dominant over finite counter-influences;

  • not meaningfully changed by context;

  • not dependent on relations for its observable effect.

The hypothesis rejects this kind of absoluteness as an ordinary feature of observable dynamic reality.

It does not reject invariants, conservation laws, stable structures, or useful idealizations.

Absolute Dynamic Identity

Absolute dynamic identity means total identity of two dynamic factors over time.

It would require more than equality of magnitude or equality at one moment. It would require preserved identity of:

  • relevant parameters;

  • relations;

  • constraints;

  • histories;

  • exposure to perturbations;

  • boundary conditions;

  • future state evolution;

  • contextual role inside the system.

The hypothesis treats absolute dynamic identity as a limiting abstraction in open systems, not as an ordinary stable condition.

Boundary Condition

A boundary condition is a condition that constrains how a system or factor behaves at the edge of a domain, interval, environment, or model.

Boundary conditions matter because two factors that are initially equal may evolve differently if their boundaries differ.

In this program, boundary conditions are part of the preservation problem:

If the boundary conditions are not preserved symmetrically, strong identity cannot simply be assumed.

Context

Context means the surrounding conditions, relations, constraints, and influences that shape the role or behavior of a factor.

A factor is not interpreted in isolation. Its effect depends on context.

The hypothesis uses context to reject the idea that a driving factor can be fully understood as an isolated absolute.

Difference

Difference is any relevant distinction between factors, states, relations, conditions, trajectories, perturbations, or histories.

The program treats difference as a condition of observable dynamics.

Difference does not always mean instability or disorder. It may also be the basis of structure, relation, measurement, interaction, and transformation.

Distinguishable Factors

Two factors are distinguishable if the model or observation treats them as two rather than one.

This matters because absolute identity between distinguishable factors is stronger than identity of a factor with itself.

The hypothesis asks:

Can two distinguishable dynamic factors preserve total identity over time under open conditions?

Driving Factor

A driving factor is any influence, interaction, constraint, gradient, field condition, instability, asymmetry, or causal structure that contributes to change of state or direction of a dynamic process.

This term is broader than force.

It is used when the strict physical term force would be too narrow or theory-dependent.

Examples may include:

  • a physical force;

  • a field condition;

  • a pressure gradient;

  • a constraint;

  • an instability;

  • an asymmetry;

  • a perturbing influence;

  • a causal relation;

  • a state-transition rule.

Driving Force

A driving force is a more intuitive phrase for a factor that pushes, directs, causes, or contributes to change.

In strict physical contexts, this phrase must be used carefully.

The program prefers driving factor when speaking across physics, mathematics, and philosophy.

Dynamic Factor

A dynamic factor is a factor whose state, influence, role, or relation is considered across time or state evolution.

A dynamic factor is not merely a static object. It is examined through change, persistence, relation, and possible divergence.

Dynamic Identity

Dynamic identity means identity considered across time or state evolution.

It is stronger than equality at one moment.

Dynamic identity asks whether a factor, or a relation between factors, remains the same through a process.

In this program, dynamic identity is always tied to preservation conditions.

Dynamic Non-Absoluteness

Dynamic non-absoluteness is the principle that observable driving factors are not absolutely isolated, not absolutely self-sufficient, and not absolutely independent of relations.

The principle states:

Observable dynamics is relational. A driving factor becomes meaningful through interaction, constraint, context, opposition, scale, and time.

Dynamic non-absoluteness does not deny stability or lawfulness. It denies that dynamic reality is ordinarily made of isolated absolutes.

Dynamic Non-Identity

Dynamic non-identity is the principle that two initially equal or similar dynamic factors do not automatically preserve strong identity over time.

The principle states:

Equality at a moment is not identity over time. Identity over time requires preserved conditions. Open systems do not automatically preserve those conditions.

Dynamic non-identity is the time-oriented part of dynamic non-absoluteness.

Equality

Equality means equivalence under a selected parameter or relation.

Equality may refer to:

  • equal magnitude;

  • equal direction;

  • equal measured value;

  • equal energy contribution;

  • equal state class;

  • equal modeled role;

  • equality under a defined equivalence relation.

The hypothesis distinguishes equality from identity.

Equality is usually parameter-bound. Identity over time is condition-bound.

Equivalence Relation

An equivalence relation is a rule under which two factors are considered equivalent for a specific purpose.

In the formal development of the hypothesis, equivalence relation is essential.

Two factors may be equivalent under one relation and not equivalent under another.

For example, two forces may have equal magnitude but different directions, histories, or contextual roles.

Exposure

Exposure means the way a factor is subject to influence from conditions, perturbations, relations, or environment.

Two factors may begin equal but diverge because their exposure differs.

Exposure is central to open-system reasoning.

Force

Force is a strict term in physics and should not be used carelessly.

In this program, force may mean a physical quantity within a specific theory, but broader statements should usually use driving factor instead.

The hypothesis is not a claim that physics has missed a new force.

Identity

Identity means sameness.

In this program, identity must always be qualified:

  • identity of value;

  • identity of state;

  • identity of relation;

  • identity of role;

  • identity of trajectory;

  • identity over time;

  • absolute dynamic identity.

The program rejects vague identity claims. It requires each identity claim to specify what is being preserved.

Invariant

An invariant is something that remains unchanged under a specified transformation or evolution.

The hypothesis does not deny invariants.

It distinguishes invariance from total dynamic identity.

Two factors may share an invariant while differing in relation, exposure, context, or trajectory.

Isolated System

An isolated system is a system treated as having no relevant exchange or influence with an environment.

In practice, isolation is often an idealization.

The hypothesis focuses mainly on open systems, but isolated-system models may be useful as contrast cases.

Limiting Abstraction

A limiting abstraction is an ideal concept useful for thought, modeling, or boundary reasoning, but not necessarily an ordinary observable reality.

In this program, absolute force and absolute dynamic identity are treated as limiting abstractions unless their preservation conditions are explicitly defined.

Local Equality

Local equality is equality within a restricted scope.

It may be equality:

  • at one time;

  • at one position;

  • under one measurement;

  • under one model assumption;

  • relative to one selected parameter.

Local equality should not be confused with total identity.

Momentary Equality

Momentary equality is equality at a specific moment of observation.

It does not imply equality before or after that moment.

It also does not imply identity of history, relation, or future development.

Open Reality

Open reality refers to observable reality as a field of interacting systems, influences, conditions, and relations rather than perfectly sealed ideal objects.

The phrase is philosophical rather than strictly technical.

It expresses the background intuition that real observed systems are usually embedded in wider fields of influence.

Open System

An open system is a system that exchanges or receives relevant influence from outside its selected boundary.

This may include exchange of:

  • energy;

  • matter;

  • information;

  • constraints;

  • perturbations;

  • boundary effects;

  • causal influences.

The hypothesis uses open systems to explain why strong identity over time is not automatically preserved.

Parameter-Bound Equality

Parameter-bound equality means equality that holds only with respect to a specified parameter.

For example, two factors may be equal in magnitude but not equal in direction, context, relation, or evolution.

This distinction protects the hypothesis from confusing measurement equality with total sameness.

Perturbation

A perturbation is a disturbance, influence, fluctuation, or modification that can affect the state or evolution of a factor or system.

Perturbations may be small or large.

The hypothesis does not require every perturbation to produce dramatic divergence. It only requires that relevant perturbations matter for claims of strong identity.

Preservation Condition

A preservation condition is a condition that must remain in place for equality, equivalence, identity, stability, or invariance to continue over time.

This is one of the core terms of the program.

The central formula is:

Identity over time requires preserved conditions.

If the preservation condition fails, identity cannot be assumed.

Preservation Mechanism

A preservation mechanism is a structure, rule, constraint, feedback process, symmetry, invariant, or stabilizing feature that maintains equivalence or identity across time.

The hypothesis allows preservation mechanisms.

It does not claim that all equalities immediately break. It says that strong identity must be supported by something; it is not automatic in open systems.

Relation

A relation is any connection, dependency, opposition, constraint, interaction, or structural placement through which a factor becomes meaningful inside a system.

Dynamic non-absoluteness treats relation as primary for observable dynamics.

A factor without relation may be thinkable as an abstraction, but it is not an ordinary observable driver inside a dynamic system.

Relational Dynamics

Relational dynamics means dynamics understood through interactions and dependencies rather than through isolated absolutes.

The hypothesis belongs to relational dynamics because it treats driving factors as meaningful through context, relation, and system structure.

Stability

Stability means persistence, return, boundedness, or resistance to disturbance under specified conditions.

Stability is not the same as absolute identity.

A system may be stable while still containing dynamic non-identity among its factors.

Strong Identity

Strong identity means identity across a richer set of properties than a single measurement.

Strong identity may include:

  • values;

  • relations;

  • histories;

  • exposure;

  • trajectories;

  • roles;

  • transition behavior.

The stronger the identity claim, the stronger the preservation conditions must be.

Symmetry

Symmetry means preservation of structure under transformation, comparison, or condition mapping.

In this program, symmetry matters because identity over time depends on preserved relevant symmetry.

Symmetry is not rejected. It is treated as a condition of identity.

Symmetry of Conditions

Symmetry of conditions means that two factors are subject to equivalent relevant conditions.

This may include equivalent:

  • boundaries;

  • perturbations;

  • relations;

  • constraints;

  • histories;

  • transition rules;

  • environmental inputs.

If symmetry of conditions is broken, strong dynamic identity becomes unstable or unsupported.

Time Interval

A time interval is the duration over which equality or identity is being evaluated.

The hypothesis is most meaningful across intervals, not instants.

At one instant, equality may be easy to define. Across time, identity becomes a preservation problem.

Trajectory

A trajectory is the path of a factor or system through state space, time, or modeled evolution.

Two factors may have equal values at one moment but different trajectories.

Dynamic identity requires more than momentary intersection of trajectories.

Weak, Medium, and Strong Claims

The hypothesis should be separated into claim levels.

Weak Claim

Equality at a moment does not imply identity over time.

Medium Claim

Identity over time requires preservation of relevant conditions.

Strong Claim

In open systems, absolute dynamic identity between distinct driving factors is a limiting abstraction rather than an ordinary stable reality.

Controlled Usage Rule

Future documents in this series should follow one rule:

Never use equality, identity, force, absolute, or open system without specifying the relevant scope.

This rule protects the research program from ambiguity.

Closing Summary

The controlled vocabulary of this program can be reduced to one chain:

Driving factors exist through relations. Equality is parameter-bound. Identity over time is condition-bound. Open systems expose conditions to difference. Therefore, absolute dynamic identity is not automatic and should be treated as a limiting abstraction unless its preservation mechanism is defined.
